Chapter Sections

Topics

Two goods are perfect substitutes when the consumer regards them as identical — always willing to trade at a constant rate. The classic example is Exxon vs. Mobil gasoline: a gallon is a gallon regardless of brand.

Indifference curves are straight, parallel, downward-sloping lines. The MRS is constant everywhere. The utility function takes the linear form \(U(X,Y) = aX + bY\), with \(\text{MRS} = a/b\) at every bundle.

Real-World ApplicationStore brands vs. name brands. If consumers treat them as identical, the only winning strategy is price-matching or genuine differentiation — otherwise consumers buy exclusively the cheaper option.

Two goods are perfect complements when they must be consumed in fixed proportions. Extra units of one without the other add zero utility. The canonical example is left and right shoes.

Indifference curves are L-shaped, with the kink on the ray \(X/a = Y/b\). The utility function is \(U(X,Y) = \min(X/a,\; Y/b)\). The MRS is undefined at the kink.

Real-World ApplicationThe razor-and-blade model — Gillette, printers and ink cartridges, iPhone and the App Store. Controlling one complement lets a firm extract value through the other.

A neutral good provides zero marginal utility — consuming more or less of it leaves utility unchanged. Indifference curves are vertical lines. Utility rises only when the valued good X increases, regardless of how much Y the consumer holds.

A bad confers negative marginal utility — more of it makes the consumer worse off. Examples include pollution, noise, and commute time. Indifference curves are upward sloping: as bad Y increases, the consumer needs more of good X to stay at the same utility level.

Policy RelevanceCompensation for workers facing longer commutes and valuation of environmental amenities both rely on this upward-sloping IC framework.

The budget constraint \(P_x X + P_y Y = I\) describes all bundles the consumer can afford when spending all income. The slope equals \(-P_x/P_y\) — the opportunity cost of X measured in units of Y.

Income change → parallel shift: both intercepts move proportionally, slope unchanged.
Price change → pivot: one intercept is fixed, the other moves, slope changes.

Application: Fuel Tax vs. Income TaxBoth policies can raise the same revenue. A fuel tax pivots the budget line — specifically discouraging driving. An income tax shifts it in parallel — reducing all consumption proportionally. Only the pivot changes relative prices.

Two conditions must hold simultaneously at the optimal bundle \((X^*, Y^*)\):

Budget exhaustion: \(P_x X^* + P_y Y^* = I\). Unspent income with positive marginal utility is always wasteful.

Tangency: \(P_x / P_y = MU_x / MU_y\). The market exchange rate equals the consumer's personal exchange rate. If they differ, a reallocation along the budget line raises utility.

Equivalently, the bang-for-the-buck condition requires \(MU_x / P_x = MU_y / P_y\). If the ratio is higher for good X, shift spending toward X until the ratios equalise.

For \(U(X,Y) = X^\alpha Y^\beta\), the demand functions are:

\[X^* = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta} \cdot \frac{I}{P_x}, \qquad Y^* = \frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta} \cdot \frac{I}{P_y}\]

The consumer spends a fixed share of income on each good regardless of prices. This fixed-expenditure-share property is one of the most empirically useful results in consumer theory.

Exam Prep

Five Common Mistakes

Watch out for these

  • Drawing a parallel shift when a price changes. A price change pivots the line around one fixed intercept. Only an income change produces a parallel shift.
  • Applying tangency to complements or substitutes. Use the kink condition \(X/a = Y/b\) for perfect complements. Compare \(MU/P\) ratios for perfect substitutes.
  • Placing the optimum off the budget line. The optimal bundle always lies exactly on the budget line — all income must be exhausted.
  • Getting the MRS direction wrong. The MRS equals \(MU_x / MU_y\), not the inverse. Inverting it is one of the most common algebraic errors.
  • Thinking higher \(MU_x/P_x\) means spend less on X. The opposite is true — shift spending toward X until the ratios equalise.

For each type, state the IC shape and give a one-sentence economic justification.

(a) Perfect substitutes: Straight, parallel, downward-sloping lines. The MRS is constant — the consumer's willingness to trade never changes regardless of quantities consumed.

(b) Perfect complements: L-shaped curves. Extra units of one good without the matching other good add zero utility, creating horizontal and vertical arms.

(c) Neutral good (Y): Vertical straight lines. Utility depends only on X, so any level of Y at fixed X gives the same utility.

(d) Economic bad (Y): Upward sloping. More of the bad reduces utility, so the consumer needs more of good X to stay on the same indifference curve.

(c) \(U = X\) — Y is a neutral good. ICs are vertical lines (constant X, any Y). Utility increases only when X increases.

(d) \(U = X - Y^2\) — since \(\partial U / \partial Y = -2Y < 0\) for \(Y > 0\), good Y is an economic bad. ICs slope upward.

Parallel shift: Both intercepts change proportionally and the slope is unchanged. Caused by an income change with prices fixed. Example: a salary increase shifts the budget line outward — the consumer can afford more of everything at unchanged relative prices.

Pivot: One intercept is fixed while the other moves, and the slope changes. Caused by a price change for one good. Example: a rise in petrol prices pivots the budget line inward around the non-petrol intercept — petrol becomes relatively more expensive without changing the price of other goods.

Condition 1 — Budget exhausted: \(P_x X^* + P_y Y^* = I\). If income is left unspent and both goods have positive marginal utility, the consumer could buy more of either and reach a higher indifference curve. Unspent income proves the consumer is not optimising.

Condition 2 — Tangency: \(P_x / P_y = MU_x / MU_y\). If the market exchange rate differs from the consumer's personal trade-off rate (MRS), a reallocation along the budget line increases utility. Only at the tangency point is it impossible to improve — any move along the line drops the consumer to a lower IC.

The bang-for-the-buck condition states \(MU_x / P_x = MU_y / P_y\): at the optimum, the extra utility per dollar spent must be equal across all goods. It follows from rearranging the tangency condition \(P_x/P_y = MU_x/MU_y\).

Business example: if a marketing manager finds that the last dollar spent on digital ads generates more sales than the last dollar on TV ads, she should shift budget from TV toward digital. She keeps reallocating until the marginal return per dollar is equalised across all channels.

She is not at an optimum. Since \(MU_x / P_x = 8 > 5 = MU_y / P_y\), good X yields more utility per dollar than good Y. She should increase spending on X and decrease spending on Y.

As she reallocates, diminishing marginal utility will lower \(MU_x\) and raise \(MU_y\), eventually bringing the two ratios into equality at the optimum.

She is describing perfect substitutes. A constant relative valuation means a constant MRS — the hallmark of linear indifference curves.

If she always values one unit of streaming at twice one unit of coffee, the utility function is \(U = \text{coffee} + 2 \cdot \text{streaming}\). The ICs are straight parallel lines with slope \(-1/2\).

If the bundle lies strictly inside the constraint, some income is unspent. Since both goods have positive marginal utility, the consumer could spend that remaining income on more of either good and reach a higher indifference curve. Any interior bundle is therefore not optimal.

If one good is a bad (negative marginal utility), the consumer wants to consume zero of it. The optimum is at the corner of the budget line where the bad is set to zero — still on the budget line, not inside it.

This holds for homothetic utility functions. For Cobb-Douglas \(U = X^\alpha Y^\beta\), the demand functions are \(X^* = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta} \cdot \frac{I}{P_x}\) and \(Y^* = \frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta} \cdot \frac{I}{P_y}\) — both linear in income. Doubling I exactly doubles both demands.

The property is called homotheticity: optimal demands scale proportionally with income at all price levels.
